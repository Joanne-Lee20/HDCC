> GLOSSARY |

Helpful vocabulary to know when engaging with discussions in AI.

Active Learning:

Machine learning system interacting with a human to improve its accuracy. Click here for more information.

Adversarial Attacks:

Manipulating input data to mislead AI models, exposing their vulnerabilities.

AI Bias Mitigation:

Efforts to identify and minimize biases in AI systems to ensure fairness and equity. Click here for an in-depth report.

AI Ethics:

Ethical considerations and guidelines governing the development and use of AI technologies. Click here to watch a video about this.

AI Positivity:

The optimistic view that artificial intelligence technologies will have a positive impact on society, emphasizing their potential benefits.

AI Research Paper:

Academic or professional document detailing research findings, methodologies, and advancements in the field of AI.

Artificial Intelligence (AI):

Simulating human intelligence in machines, enabling them to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. Click here for an overview of AI.

Bias in AI:

Unfair and discriminatory behavior in AI systems, often reflecting biases present in the training data.

Chatbot:

A computer program designed to simulate conversation with human users, often used for customer service or information retrieval. Click here to learn about Amazon Lex, a prominent customer service chatbot builder.

Character-level Generative Models:

Models generating text character by character, often used for creative text generation.

Classification Algorithms:

Machine learning algorithms used for categorizing data into predefined classes or labels.

Constitutional AI:

AI systems designed to uphold and respect ethical principles, similar to how a constitution guides a nation's governance.

Conversational AI:

Technology that enables machines to engage in natural conversations with users, often powered by AI algorithms.

Crowdsourcing:

Gathering information or input from a large number of people, often online. Click here for an article about crowdsourcing.

Data Acquisition:

Gathering information from various sources to build a dataset.

Data Augmentation:

Enhancing a dataset by adding more varied or modified examples.

Data Cleaning:

Identifying and correcting errors or inconsistencies in a dataset.

Data Curation:

The process of organizing, cleaning, and managing data to ensure its accuracy and usefulness for analysis or machine learning.

Data Discovery:

Identifying and exploring new patterns or insights within existing data.

Data Generation:

Creating new data points using algorithms or simulations.

Data Improvement:

Enhancing data quality by correcting errors or inconsistencies.

Data Integration:

Combining data from different sources to provide a unified view.

Data Labeling:

Assigning relevant tags or categories to data for machine learning training.

Data Lake:

A centralized repository that can store vast amounts of raw data in its native format.

Data Validation:

Ensuring data accuracy, completeness, and reliability.

Dataset Shift:

Changes in the data distribution affecting model performance.

Deep Learning:

Subset of machine learning using neural networks with multiple layers to analyze patterns in large datasets. Click here for an introductory lecture about this.

Deepfaking:

Technology using deep learning to create realistic-looking fake videos or images, often involving faces or voices.

Dialog System:

Software or AI that facilitates a conversation between a user and a machine, like a chatbot or virtual assistant.

Face Recognition:

Technology enabling machines to identify or verify individuals by analyzing facial features.

Fine-Tuning vs. Transfer Learning:

Adjusting a pre-trained model on specific data versus using a pre-trained model as a starting point for a new task.

Generative AI:

AI systems capable of creating new content, such as images, text, or music. Click here to see a list of generative AI tools, and click here to see how Google is using generative AI.

GPTZero:

A hypothetical term; perhaps you meant GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer), a popular language processing AI model developed by OpenAI.

Image Generation:

Creating images through AI algorithms, commonly used in art and design applications.

Interpretability in AI:

Making AI models understandable and transparent, crucial for trust and accountability.

Machine Learning (ML):

Subset of AI focusing on systems that learn from data and improve their performance over time. For a general overview, click here to watch a brief video. For in-depth information, click here for a playlist of lectures about machine learning.

Model Collapse:

A situation in training AI models where the model converges to generating a limited set of responses, losing diversity and creativity in its outputs.

Multilingual AI:

AI systems capable of understanding, processing, or generating content in multiple languages.

Natural Language Processing (NLP):

AI technology enabling machines to understand, interpret, and generate human language.

Neural Networks:

Computing systems inspired by the human brain, used in deep learning for pattern recognition and decision-making.

Object Detection:

Technology used in computer vision to identify and locate objects within images or videos.

OpenAI:

An organization developing artificial intelligence technologies and promoting their safe and ethical use. Click here to view the OpenAI website.

Overfitting:

When a machine learning model learns the training data too well, capturing noise and outliers, which can lead to poor performance on new, unseen data.

Primary Data:

Information collected firsthand from original sources.

Prompt Decay:

The phenomenon in AI language models where the effectiveness of a prompt diminishes over time as the model's parameters are updated.

Quantum Machine Learning:

Intersection of quantum computing and machine learning, exploring algorithms on quantum computers.

Re-labeling:

Updating or changing labels assigned to data points, often for refining machine learning models.

Reinforcement Learning:

Machine learning paradigm where algorithms learn by interacting with an environment, receiving rewards or penalties for their actions.

Reinforcement Learning Agent:

AI entity that learns to make decisions by interacting with an environment and receiving rewards or punishments.

Robust Model Training:

Training models to perform well under various conditions and inputs.

Robustness in AI:

Ensuring AI models perform reliably under various conditions and inputs, guarding against errors.

Secondary Data:

Data collected by someone other than the user for a different purpose.

Self-Supervised Learning:

Learning paradigm where models generate labels or supervisory signals from the data itself. Click here for more details.

Semi-Supervised Learning:

Training machine learning models using a mix of labeled and unlabeled data. Click here for more information and examples.

Semi-Unsupervised Learning:

Training machine learning models primarily with unlabeled data, supplemented by a small amount of labeled data.

Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq) Model:

Neural network architecture used for tasks where the input and output are sequences, such as language translation.

Speech-to-Text (STT):

Technology transforming spoken words into written text using AI algorithms. Click here for more information.

Supervised Learning:

Machine learning technique where models are trained on labeled data, learning to map inputs to desired outputs. Click here to learn more.

Text Generation:

Creating textual content using algorithms, often seen in chatbots and creative writing applications.

Text-to-Speech (TTS):

Technology converting written text into spoken words using AI algorithms. Click to learn about NaturalReader, an AI text-to-speech service.

Training Data:

The data used to teach machine learning algorithms and models, enabling them to make predictions or decisions.

Unsupervised Learning:

Machine learning approach where algorithms find patterns and relationships in data without labeled responses. Click here to learn more.

Variational Autoencoder (VAE):

Neural network model used for unsupervised learning, generating new data points similar to the training data. Click here for a video about this.

Weak Supervision:

Training models with partially labeled or noisy data. (definition via ChatGPT)

Word-level Generative Models:

Models generating text at the word level, creating coherent sentences and paragraphs.

Zero-Shot Learning:

Model's ability to recognize or generate examples of classes it has never seen during training.
